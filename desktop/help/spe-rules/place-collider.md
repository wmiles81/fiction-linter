## Place Collider

The **Place Collider** flags location names that language models default to — names so generic they could appear in any AI-generated story and therefore create no sense of place in yours.

### What it catches

**Generic town names** — pastoral American-style small towns with compound nature words: Willow Creek, Maplewood, Riverside, Oakwood, Pinehurst, Cedar Falls. The Place Collider also catches common patterns: anything ending in Harbor (Crescent Harbor, Silver Harbor), Springs (Crystal Springs, Willow Springs), Heights (Sterling Heights, Maple Heights), Glen, Bay, Ridge, and Vale.

**Generic city names** — slightly more urban but equally interchangeable: New Haven, Port Sterling, Silverton, Ashbourne, Blackwater, Ravenwood, Thornwood.

**Fantasy locations** — kingdoms, realms, and forests that appear verbatim across thousands of AI-generated stories: Kingdom of Eldoria, Kingdom of Arcadia, The Shadowlands, The Darklands, The Borderlands.

### Why place names matter

A place name is a promise to the reader about what kind of world this is. "Willow Creek" promises a soft-focus contemporary romance in a Hallmark-movie setting, even if your story is a gritty thriller set in rural Oregon. A specific, invented name — or better, a real but unexpected geographic reference — signals that the author knows this world.

### Alternatives

When the Place Collider flags a location, consider:
- Using the real name of a place you know well enough to describe accurately
- Inventing a name rooted in your story's specific history, geography, or culture
- Describing the place without naming it at all
