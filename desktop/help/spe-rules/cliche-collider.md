## Cliche Collider

The **Cliche Collider** is the largest and most wide-ranging SPE rule set. It flags phrases that signal over-written, AI-generated, or otherwise weakened prose — grouped into several categories.

### Somatic cliches

Physical-sensation cliches that appear in fiction so often they no longer create feeling in the reader. Examples: "shiver down her spine," "heart skipped a beat," "breath hitched," "butterflies in stomach," "weak in the knees." The fix is almost always to replace the stock phrase with a specific, concrete physical detail that belongs to this scene and this character.

### AI tells

Structural patterns that mark text as AI-generated rather than human-authored. Examples: "It's worth noting that," "In many ways," "There was something about," "couldn't help but," "found herself," "seemed to." These phrases hedge, equivocate, or avoid committing to an image — habits AI models acquired from training on hedged, formal writing.

### Weak descriptors

Low-information intensifiers and qualifiers: "very," "really," "just," "quite," "rather." The fix is to delete the word entirely or replace it with a stronger, more specific word.

### Emotion tells

Words that name an emotion instead of showing it: "felt," "realized," "knew," "thought." These are not always wrong — interior monologue needs them sometimes — but the Cliche Collider flags them so you can decide consciously whether each one earns its place.

### Purple prose

Overwrought or evasive word choices that call attention to themselves: using "orbs" to mean eyes, "tresses" for hair, "digits" for fingers.

### Dialogue crutches

Overused dialogue beats: "chuckled," "smirked," "rolled her eyes."

### Severity

Higher-penalty phrases (somatic cliches, AI tells) are flagged as **warnings** or **errors**. Lower-penalty phrases (weak descriptors, dialogue crutches) are flagged as **info**.
